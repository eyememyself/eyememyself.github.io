7.2.1. Q

  • Taleb on Piketty

7.2.2. A

  • Nassim Nicholas Taleb has offered extensive and critical commentary on Thomas Piketty's work, particularly focusing on Piketty's central thesis involving the relationship between the rate of return on capital (\[r\]) and the economic growth rate (\[g\]), often summarized as \[r > g\] Taleb argues that Piketty's analysis contains fundamental flaws, both empirically and logically. He contends that Piketty's measure of inequality can be inconsistent across different population samples, especially under conditions of "fat tails," where extreme events significantly influence outcomes Taleb asserts that inequality can appear to rise over time simply due to population growth or measurement errors, rather than genuine increases in economic disparity
  • Taleb further criticizes Piketty's methodology, claiming that the use of Pareto interpolation to fill income brackets may introduce inaccuracies He also challenges the very foundation of Piketty's argument, stating that the assumption of \[r = g\] in the long term is meaningless without specifying a time horizon, and that the equality of parameters does not imply equality of integrals, especially when stochastic processes and the risk of "Black Swan" events are considered Taleb argues that \[r > g\] is a necessity most of the time, not an equilibrium condition, and that the rate of return on capital (\[r\]) is influenced by the level of capital (\[K\]), which itself affects the growth rate (\[g\])
  • Beyond the technical critique, Taleb emphasizes a philosophical and ethical dimension. He argues that the real issue is not inequality itself, but the lack of "skin in the game" among the wealthy and powerful According to Taleb, people resent those who accumulate wealth not through risk-taking and genuine contribution, but through rent-seeking, regulatory capture, or privileged positions that shield them from risk He distinguishes between "tolerable" inequality, such as that of entrepreneurs or artists who bear personal risk, and "intolerable" inequality, where individuals benefit from the system without facing its consequences Taleb suggests that true equality should be measured not by static snapshots of wealth distribution, but by dynamic measures that account for lifetime income mobility and the risk of falling from the top He proposes that a society is more equal when the wealthy are subject to the risk of losing their status, ensuring a more dynamic and fair system
